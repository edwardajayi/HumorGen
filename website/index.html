Overview

The whole story, in one paragraph.

What we do

Distill six comedic personas into a 7B student.

A teacher ensemble (Kimi-K2 and Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct) generates 24 candidates per headline via CSF — four per persona × six personas. A 7B student (Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct) is fine-tuned on top-ranked jokes from a HumorRank Bradley–Terry tournament judged by Llama 3.3-70B.

What we find

CSF data curation beats scale — and alignment hits a ceiling.

HumorGen-SFT-7B ranks among the strongest open-weight models and outperforms models many times its size, competitive with frontier systems. DPO matches SFT; O-GRPO trails both. When SFT data is diverse and well-curated, preference optimization yields no significant gains — a data quality ceiling.

The problem

Funny lives at the edges of the distribution.

Language models struggle to be funny — not because they lack knowledge of humor, but because of how they are trained.

The objective mismatch

Next-token prediction rewards the median.

Standard training maximizes the likelihood of the next word. The most probable continuation is always the safest, most generic one — so the model is pulled toward the center of the distribution, away from anything surprising.

What comedy demands

Incongruity lives in the tail.

Humor lives at the edges: the unexpected turn of phrase, the precise word that collapses two meanings at once, the observation that makes a situation suddenly absurd. The funniest line is rarely the most likely one.

Training a model to "be funny" with a single instruction does not work — it produces outputs that scan as jokes but fail to land. HumorGen's premise: a single headline supports multiple valid comedic interpretations. Teach the model that diversity, and the laughs follow.

Our approach

The Cognitive Synergy Framework.

A Mixture-of-Thought method that structures humor generation as an ensemble of six cognitive personas, each grounded in psychological theory.

Rather than a single generation path, CSF produces 24 parallel candidates per headline — four per persona × six personas — creating a diverse pool by construction. A HumorRank pairwise tournament (Llama 3.3-70B judge, Bradley–Terry) selects top jokes for SFT distillation into a 7B student.

Mixture-of-Thought

24 candidates, not one.

A teacher ensemble runs six persona-conditioned paths in parallel, generating four candidates per persona (24 per headline). This routes generation into low-probability, high-variance regions where humor lives.

Silver teacher distillation

Rank, then distill.

Llama 3.3-70B judges all 24 candidates via pairwise comparison; Bradley–Terry aggregation yields Elo ratings. Top-ranked jokes become SFT targets for the 7B student.

Training pipeline

From twenty-four candidates to one funny model.

01

Teacher generation

Kimi-K2 and Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct generate 24 candidates per headline (4 per persona × 6 personas) on SemEval-2026 MWAHAHA — 1,200 prompts, ~28,800 raw candidates.

02

Ranking & SFT

Llama 3.3-70B-Instruct judges all candidates pairwise; HumorRank Bradley–Terry aggregation yields Elo ratings. Top-ranked jokes fine-tune Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (LoRA r=16, Unsloth). Think variants add Cognitive Synergy Distillation (CSD) reasoning traces.

03

Alignment ablation

We test whether preference optimization helps beyond SFT: DPO (β = 0.1, pairwise) and O-GRPO (G = 24, offline group-relative). Result: DPO matches SFT; O-GRPO trails both — neither improves over a well-curated SFT baseline.

DPO

Direct Preference Optimization

  • Pairwise preferences from HumorRank Elo rankings: top-5 vs bottom-5 per prompt.
  • β = 0.1; initialized from SFT checkpoint.
  • Performance similar to SFT — 95% CIs overlap on HTB and SemEval.
  • HumorGen-SFT-7B and HumorGen-DPO-7B cluster at ranks 3–4 on HTB.
O-GRPO · offline

Offline Group Relative Policy Optimization

  • All 24 candidates per prompt ranked once via Bradley–Terry; group-relative advantages from static scores.
  • G = 24; offline (no live rollout during training).
  • Consistently underperforms SFT and DPO (e.g. rank 7th on HTB).
  • Skewed advantage distribution penalizes weaker samples more than it reinforces strong ones.
key finding · data quality ceiling

Targeted CSF data curation matters more than model scale for humor: HumorGen-SFT-7B outperforms models 4–18× larger and is competitive with frontier systems. When SFT data is diverse and well-curated, preference optimization (DPO, O-GRPO) yields no significant gains. Reasoning-augmented (Think) variants can reduce judged funniness — the “explainer trap.”

CLEF 2026 JOKER · Task 4

Extending to constrained humor.

The CSF isn't limited to open-ended generation. JOKER Task 4 is harder: given a pun word and two required semantic senses, generate a pun-brief — a sentence that satisfies both senses and still reads as funny. The model must navigate strict lexical constraints without losing the laugh.

Stage 1 — Humor prior

QLoRA fine-tuning on ~12k CSF-curated English headline jokes (SemEval MWAHAHA train set). Produces HumorGen-SFT-14B and HumorGen-SFT-32B — general humor checkpoints before any JOKER data.

Stage 2a — English JOKER

English student fine-tunes directly on 3,985 monolingual JOKER CSF examples. Bridges open-ended headline humor → constrained pun-brief generation.

Stage 2b — Multilingual warm-up

French and Spanish branch from Stage 1 into a shared warm-up on 11,038 combined EN+FR+ES JOKER examples — broader pun-brief exposure before per-language specialization.

Stage 3 — FR / ES specialization

Per-language fine-tuning on monolingual JOKER CSF sets (FR: 3,952 rows; ES: 3,101 rows) at reduced learning rate. Six open-weight adapters released (EN/FR/ES × 14B/32B).

A valid pun-brief must use the pun word, activate both senses, read naturally in the target language, and still be funny. Our JOKER ablation finds that CSF guidance at test time dominates distillation: Kimi-CSF leads all three language tracks; student models transfer structural competence but not the multi-candidate search breadth of the full CSF pipeline.

Usage

Load an adapter in four lines.

Every model is a PEFT LoRA adapter — load the base, apply the adapter, generate.

from transformers import AutoModelForCausalLM, AutoTokenizer
from peft import PeftModel
import torch

tokenizer = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained("Qwen/Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct")
model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained(
    "Qwen/Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct", torch_dtype=torch.bfloat16, device_map="auto")
model = PeftModel.from_pretrained(model, "Jayi2424/HumorGen_SFT_7B")

headline = "Scientists discover that staring at spreadsheets increases sadness by 94%"
prompt = (
    "<|im_start|>system\nYou are a comedy writer. Write one sharp, witty joke for the headline.\n<|im_end|>\n"
    f"<|im_start|>user\n{headline}<|im_end|>\n"
    "<|im_start|>assistant\n"
)
inputs  = tokenizer(prompt, return_tensors="pt").to(model.device)
outputs = model.generate(**inputs, max_new_tokens=120, temperature=0.9, top_p=0.95)
print(tokenizer.decode(outputs[0], skip_special_tokens=True))
from transformers import AutoModelForCausalLM, AutoTokenizer
from peft import PeftModel
import torch

tokenizer = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained("Qwen/Qwen3-32B")
model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained(
    "Qwen/Qwen3-32B", torch_dtype=torch.bfloat16, device_map="auto")
model = PeftModel.from_pretrained(model, "Jayi2424/HumorGen_JOKER_EN_32B")

pun_word = "vein"
sense_a = "blood vessel toward heart"
sense_b = "to no avail (in vain)"
prompt = (
    "Write a humorous text for this pun brief.\n\n"
    f"Language: English\nPun word: {pun_word}\n"
    f"Sense A: {sense_a}\nSense B: {sense_b}\n"
)
inputs  = tokenizer(prompt, return_tensors="pt").to(model.device)
outputs = model.generate(**inputs, max_new_tokens=80, temperature=0.8, top_p=0.95)
print(tokenizer.decode(outputs[0], skip_special_tokens=True))
